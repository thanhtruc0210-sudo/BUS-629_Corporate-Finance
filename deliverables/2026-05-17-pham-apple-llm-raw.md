## Performance Ratios

Apple's market-value performance metrics are extraordinary by any standard. Market capitalization of $3,773,977M (14,773.26M shares × $255.46) supports an **MVA of $3,700,244M** (~$3.7T), the gap between market value and book equity ($73,733M). The **market-to-book multiple of 51.18x** says equity holders pay roughly 51 dollars for every dollar of recorded book value — a multiple GAAP simply cannot justify on the assets side. The explanation is structural: GAAP does not capitalize Apple's brand, installed base, services ecosystem, or the value-accretive buyback machinery that has shrunk equity faster than retained earnings could grow it. The balance sheet records the inputs of operations; the market prices the cash-flow engine those operations produce.

**EVA of $99,880.5M** corroborates the market's view with an internal yardstick. Apple's after-tax operating income of $112,010M exceeds the capital charge of $12,129.5M (8.5% × prior-year capitalization of $142,700M) by nearly $100B per year. Read together, MVA, M/B, and EVA tell one coherent story: Apple earns far above its cost of capital, the market knows it, and book equity is the wrong scale on which to value the firm.

## Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios are uniformly elite, but their magnitude is partly a function of the denominator. **ROA of 30.69%** ($112,010M after-tax operating income ÷ $364,980M start-of-year assets) is industry-leading: Apple earns nearly 31 cents of after-tax operating profit per dollar of beginning-of-year assets. The averaged variant, **ROA [avg] of 30.93%**, smooths the modest asset contraction (assets fell from $364,980M to $359,241M) and is essentially unchanged — evidence the asset base is genuinely productive, not artificially shrunk through one-off divestitures.

**ROC of 78.49%** ($112,010M ÷ $142,700M start-of-year capitalization of long-term debt plus equity) shows the long-term capital structure earns nearly 80 cents per dollar invested. **ROC [avg] of 76.00%** confirms the stability. These are not ratios sustainable under perfect competition; they are the signature of deep moats.

**ROE of 196.68%** ($112,010M ÷ $56,950M start-of-year equity) is the headline-grabbing figure and the one that demands the most caution. Apple is not "twice as profitable per dollar of book equity" as a real economic matter; the figure is inflated because management spent roughly $90.7B in FY2025 alone repurchasing shares, driving the equity base toward zero. The averaged **ROE of 171.42%** remains structurally distorted. Treat ROC and ROA as the cleaner profitability signals and ROE as a hybrid metric blending operating excellence with capital-return policy. The Du Pont decomposition below quantifies how much of ROE is "real" operating return versus leverage geometry.

## Efficiency Ratios

Efficiency ratios show a tight operation. **Asset turnover of 1.140x** ($416,161M sales ÷ $364,980M prior assets) is healthy for a hardware-plus-services franchise — Apple converts every dollar of beginning assets into $1.14 of revenue. **Inventory turnover of 30.33x** ($220,960M COGS ÷ $7,286M prior inventory) translates to **just 12.04 days in inventory**, evidence of the just-in-time supply chain. Inventory shrank further during the year to $5,718M (Δ inventories +$1,400M source of cash), suggesting either tightening discipline or a deliberate run-down into a product transition; FY2026 will distinguish the two.

**Receivables turnover of 6.282x** ($416,161M sales ÷ $66,243M prior receivables) corresponds to an **average collection period of 58.10 days** ($66,243M ÷ $1,140.17M daily sales) — longer than a pure consumer retailer would tolerate but normal for a business with large enterprise, channel-partner, and carrier receivables alongside direct retail. Receivables grew from $66,243M to $72,957M during the year (Δ AR = −$7,029M in cash flow), implying collection may be lengthening at the margin — a watch item.

**Net profit margin of 26.92%** ($112,010M ÷ $416,161M) and **operating profit margin of 26.92%** are identical because interest expense is zero, which collapses the wedge between operating and net margin. These margins translate every dollar of revenue into roughly 27 cents of net income. Combined with asset turnover of 1.14x, the margin level is the dominant driver of the 30.69% ROA — confirmed mechanically in the Du Pont identity below.

## Leverage Ratios

Apple's leverage profile looks aggressive but tells a different story decomposed. **Total debt ratio of 79.48%** ($285,508M ÷ $359,241M) is well above the 60% conventional comfort line. **Long-term debt ratio of 51.51%** ($78,328M ÷ $152,061M long-term capitalization) and **long-term debt-equity of 1.062x** put long-term debt slightly above book equity.

The **leverage multiplier of 4.872x** ($359,241M assets ÷ $73,733M equity) is the key cross-link to Du Pont and the inflated ROE. Apple is not "leveraged" in the distressed sense — it is *un-equity-zed*. The denominator has been shrunk on purpose: the buyback program has cumulatively returned more capital than retained earnings have replaced, leaving retained earnings at −$19,835M (an accumulated-deficit-plus-AOCI position by reclassification, not an operating loss). The 4.872x multiplier reflects shareholder-return policy, not borrowing aggression.

**Times Interest Earned and Cash Coverage are both n/a** because reported interest expense is zero — the standard solvency-coverage ratios degenerate to the documented "n/a (no interest exp.)" guard. This is itself a leverage signal: a firm with ~$98.7B in gross debt and zero net interest expense generates enough investment income to offset its borrowing cost.

The **debt burden ratio of 1.000** ($112,010M net income ÷ $112,010M after-tax operating income) confirms there is no interest or tax wedge between operating and net profit — every dollar earned operationally arrives at the bottom line. Together, the seven leverage metrics describe a deliberately capital-light equity structure, not balance-sheet stress.

## Liquidity Ratios

On classical metrics, Apple looks illiquid. **Current ratio of 0.893** ($147,957M ÷ $165,631M) is well below the 1.5–2.0 textbook norm; **quick ratio of 0.771** ((54,697 + 72,957) ÷ 165,631); **cash ratio of 0.330** ($54,697M ÷ $165,631M); and **NWC/Assets of −4.92%** (−$17,674M ÷ $359,241M) all sit below conventional comfort levels. For most companies this would be a warning.

For Apple, the sub-1.0 current ratio should be re-framed as efficient working-capital management rather than weakness. Three structural facts support this view. First, Apple operates with **negotiated supplier financing**: $69,860M of accounts payable functions as interest-free funding from a concentrated supply base, longer in duration than its own 58-day collection cycle. Second, **operating cash generation in FY2025 was $111,482M** — Apple produces in roughly two months what it would take to cover the entire $17.7B current-liability gap. Third, the $54.7B cash and marketable securities position is already a deliberate optimization downward from $65.2B prior year as management redirected cash to the buyback program; if needed, the firm controls the buyback dial and can rebuild the cushion at will. Negative net working capital is, for Apple, a feature — not a defect — of the cash-conversion model.

## Du Pont Decomposition

The Du Pont system decomposes ROE into four levers and lets us separate operating performance from capital-structure effects.

**ROA decomposition** is the simpler half: ROA = asset turnover × operating profit margin = **1.140 × 26.92% = 30.69%**, matching direct ROA to six decimals (Validation Check 6.2 passes). The split shows margin is the larger driver, though turnover ceilings in this business model are tight — Apple's ROA is structurally a *margin* story.

**ROE decomposition** uses four levers: **leverage × asset turnover × operating profit margin × debt burden = 4.872 × 1.140 × 0.2692 × 1.000 = 149.52%**.

*Primary driver.* Of the four, leverage (4.872x) is the single largest amplifier. Asset turnover (1.140x) provides modest amplification, operating profit margin (26.92%) sizes the base level of ROA, and the debt burden (1.000) is neutral (zero interest expense leaves operating and net income identical). **Leverage is doing the heaviest lifting** in turning a 30.69% ROA into a triple-digit ROE — not because Apple borrows aggressively (total debt is roughly stable year-over-year) but because the equity denominator has been compressed by buybacks. Operationally, Apple's ROE rests on margin × turnover (the ROA core); financially, it is multiplied by a leverage figure that reflects shareholder-return policy.

*Du Pont-ROE vs. direct-ROE gap.* The Du Pont ROE (149.52%) is materially below the direct ROE (196.68%). The 47-point gap is not a calculation error; it is a deliberate time-mismatch (Validation Check 6.3). Direct ROE uses **start-of-year equity** ($56,950M); the Du Pont leverage component uses **current-year equity** ($73,733M, larger because FY2025's addition to retained earnings was $96,589M before offsetting buyback charges). Direct ROE answers "what return did shareholders earn on the equity they started the year with?" — the better economic measure for period attribution. Du Pont ROE answers "what is the steady-state return implied by today's capital structure?" — the better diagnostic for sustainability. Both are correct under their own definitions.

*Sustainability.* Margin (26.92%) and turnover (1.14x) are operationally durable. Leverage (4.87x), being a function of the buyback dial, is a policy variable; if Apple paused buybacks, equity would grow with retained earnings and ROE would compress toward roughly 2× ROA. The 196.68% ROE is therefore *real but not steady-state*.
